# Adversarial Review — INK PRD (2026-06-14)

*Methodology: cynical reviewer. Every finding cites a specific PRD/addendum location and states why it is a real risk to safely greenlighting a build — not a stylistic nitpick. Findings are ranked by impact. A "safe greenlight" means: an engineer could build this without inventing scope, and a skeptical stakeholder could not later say "you never told me that would happen."*

**Verdict:** Conditional greenlight. The PRD is unusually disciplined on vocabulary, layering, and the conflation rule — those are genuinely build-ready. But it greenlights a build whose *thesis* (developmental craft improvement) is measured by *activity* (visit frequency, feedback presence), whose *migration* (the actual launch-stakes work) hides several untested "verify" requirements behind a clone that "rides" production, and whose *scope* (62 FRs + 11 migration steps + 9 NFRs across 17 epics) is sized for a team but resourced for one builder + agents with no launch date. Build the foundation and migration spike first; do not greenlight the full P0+P1 surface as a single committed scope.

## CRITICAL

### C-1. The success metrics measure activity, not the stated thesis. (§1 Vision; §15 SM-4, SM-6, SM-7)
The Vision (§1) stakes everything on a *developmental* wedge: INK "improves working writers through structured expert feedback and progression" and is "indispensable to Afrikaans literary education." That is a claim about **writers getting better**. Not one Success Metric measures it. SM-4 measures *return visits* (≥6×/month), SM-6 measures *feedback presence* (≥50% of pieces get ≥1 response within 14 days), SM-7 measures *participation breadth*. All three are activity proxies. A platform can hit 6 visits/month, 50% response density, and three-tier challenge participation while every writer's craft stagnates — i.e., the product can fully "succeed" on its metrics while failing its entire reason to exist.
**Why this is critical:** the PRD itself warns (§15 preamble) that "counter-metrics keep the build from optimising the wrong thing" — yet the *primary* metrics already optimise the wrong thing. SM-6 specifically rewards *any* response within 14 days; the counter-metric SM-C2 admits responses can spike in quantity while quality (insig/voorstel share) falls. There is no metric for *progression actually happening* (e.g., tier promotions earned via demonstrated improvement, repeat-writer quality trajectory, feedback that writers act on). The thesis is "developmental"; the scorecard is "engaged." A skeptical stakeholder funding "literary education" is being sold reach metrics with a resonance label.
**What would fix it:** at least one primary SM that operationalizes craft growth — even a proxy like "% of active writers who publish a revised/improved follow-up after receiving voorstel/insig" or a periodic editorial-judged quality sample. As written, the PRD cannot tell you at launch+6mo whether the product did the one thing it exists to do.

### C-2. Migration's load-bearing requirements are "verify," not "build," and the riskiest data path is untested by design. (§10 MR-5; §10 "Must survive"; Addendum E; §15 SM-1)
The single most important launch-stakes fact in this PRD: subscriptions and membership records **"ride the DB clone, no import script"** (§10 Must-survive; MR-5; Addendum E). The requirement for the paying-customer data is therefore not "migrate it" but "*verify* it fires on the new host" (MR-5: "Verify subscriptions on the new host… active state, plan IDs, expiry/suspension logic fire"). This is a hope dressed as a requirement. "Ride the clone" assumes the new host's PHP/MySQL versions, WooCommerce Memberships version, PayFast gateway config, cron behaviour, and serialized-option contents all reproduce production behaviour bit-for-bit. The Addendum (build target PHP 8.3+, WP 7.0+) signals the new stack is **not** identical to the legacy site — so "ride the clone" and "modern stack" are in tension the PRD never reconciles.
**Why this is critical:** SM-1 declares "100% of active intekenings survive cutover" and "any loss is a launch blocker." But there is no defined *test* that proves a migrated subscription will correctly auto-suspend on its real expiry date weeks after cutover, or that a mid-cycle renewal computes the right new expiry. "Expiry/suspension logic fire" is asserted as verifiable but no acceptance procedure exists (you cannot fast-forward a real expiry date in a smoke test without time-mocking the membership engine, which is nowhere specified). The thing most likely to silently break post-launch — a paying member wrongly suspended, or an expired member never suspended (revenue leak) — is the thing with the weakest verification. This is the classic brownfield trap: the demo works on cutover day; the time-dependent entitlement logic breaks in week 3.
**What would fix it:** a concrete, time-mocked acceptance test in NFR-9 for the *entitlement lifecycle* (active → near-expiry → expired → suspended → renewed) against migrated records, plus a documented rollback/reconciliation plan if migrated subscriptions misbehave post-cutover. Right now MR-5 + SM-1 + NFR-9's E2E ("renewal/expiry") gesture at this but none of them defines the time dimension that is the entire risk.

### C-3. Reclassification of "several thousand" posts has no defined success bar and a fallback bucket with no exit. (§10 MR-6, Risk 2; §4.9/§3 `skryfwerk`)
MR-6 reclassifies posts into CPTs from "writers' self-assigned categories," explicitly "never hand-classified at volume," with unclassifiable posts landing in the **skryfwerk** holding bucket. Risk 2 admits the categories are "unreliable" and that this "force[s] a bounded manual bulk-edit pass" — but "bounded" is undefined (10 posts? 1,000?). The glossary calls `skryfwerk` a holding bucket; §4.9 / Risk 2 say it "must have a defined later-reclassification policy" — but no such policy exists in the PRD. SM-1 requires 100% of bydraes "survive," which they will (as rows), but says nothing about being *correctly typed*.
**Why this is critical:** "survive" and "correctly classified" are conflated into a passing metric. A migration where 30% of the library's prose lands in an invisible `skryfwerk` bucket technically scores 100% on SM-1 while the public **Ontdek** library (the thing readers return for — see C-1's return metric) is gutted. "Done" for migration is undefined: there is no threshold for acceptable misclassification, no metric for `skryfwerk` residue, and no policy for ever draining the bucket. An engineer cannot know when MR-6 is "complete," and a stakeholder cannot know if the migrated library is usable.
**What would fix it:** a measurable classification target (e.g., "≤X% of published works in `skryfwerk` at cutover; a named owner and timeline to reclassify the remainder") promoted to an SM, and a definition of "bounded" for the manual pass.

## HIGH

### H-1. "Zero English leakage" is an absolute, standing, automated bar over dependencies the PRD admits it cannot translate. (§7 NFR-1; §15 SM-2; Addendum D)
SM-2 demands "**zero** English strings on key front-end pages + user-facing emails at launch, **and stays at zero after ungated updates**." Addendum D concedes the "irreducible exposure = premium/niche plugins with no community language packs (WooCommerce Memberships, PayFast gateway, Real3D, Report Content)" defended only by "committed `.mo`." A committed `.mo` covers *known* strings; any plugin update that introduces a *new* English string, or a plugin-composed sentence (BuddyPress notification, Woo order phrasing), or a JS string in a `.json` not yet authored, breaks "zero" until someone notices. NFR-1 itself scopes "key front-end pages" — so "zero English leakage" is really "zero on the pages we crawl," which is not what SM-2's absolute language promises.
**Why this is high:** an absolute, *standing* metric ("stays at zero") that depends on third-party update behaviour is not a metric you can pass — it's a metric you can only fail later. The build can hit zero at launch and SM-2 still "fails" the first time PayFast ships a new error string. Either the bar is "zero on a defined page set at launch, with a remediation SLA for regressions" (achievable) or it's "zero forever everywhere" (impossible given the admitted irreducible exposure). The PRD wants both. A skeptical engineer will not sign up for an unbounded standing absolute against vendors they don't control.
**What would fix it:** restate SM-2 as a launch gate (zero on enumerated surfaces) plus a regression SLA (new leak → authored + redeployed within N days), and explicitly accept that the *window* between an ungated update and remediation is non-zero.

### H-2. The custom-form decisions quietly expanded scope into security-, spam-, and POPIA-sensitive territory with no requirements. (FR-61; §8 moderation; §9; OQ-4/OQ-8)
Two "resolved" open questions (OQ-4, OQ-8) decided to build **custom `ink-core` forms** for both Contact (FR-61) and the moderation report path (§8) "to avoid CF7/Fluent Forms dependency." This is framed as a simplification. It is the opposite: the PRD just took on writing two public, unauthenticated-input-accepting forms by hand, and specified *zero* requirements for spam protection (no captcha/honeypot/rate-limiting mentioned), email deliverability (a contact form that emails staff — via what? SMTP config is unmentioned), validation, or abuse handling. Mature form plugins exist precisely because these are non-trivial. The report form additionally feeds the moderation flow whose SLA and escalation owner are *still deferred* (OQ-4 policy remainder) — so a custom intake exists with no defined downstream.
**Why this is high:** "decided, no dependency" reads as closed scope, but it's open scope with hidden cost. A public contact form with no spam strategy is a guaranteed spam vector on a real site; a report form with no SLA is a moderation black hole. The PRD smoothed a real build-vs-buy tension to "neutral/resolved" without pricing the build side.
**What would fix it:** FRs for spam mitigation, rate-limiting, deliverability/SMTP, and validation on the custom forms; and block the report form behind a defined moderation SLA/owner before it ships.

### H-3. Scope vastly exceeds stated resourcing, and the PRD's own "no launch date" removes the only forcing function that would keep it honest. (§14 MVP; §0 audience; §1 "why now")
Launch scope (§14.1) = P0 **+** P1: that is ~50 of 62 FRs, MR-1 through MR-11, all 9 NFRs, across the "17-epic / ~109-story" breakdown (§0). The named team (§0) is "the builder (Cobus) and the AI build agents." §1 explicitly records "no fixed launch date is set." So the resourcing is one builder, and the schedule has no constraint. A 109-story, 17-epic brownfield rebuild *with a live migration* delivered by a solo builder against no deadline is not an MVP — it's an open-ended program. The label "MVP Scope" on §14 is doing a lot of unearned work: an MVP is the *smallest* thing that validates the thesis, and almost nothing here is cut to that test.
**Why this is high:** the "why now" (§1) identifies the *actual* minimal value: "the single change that lifts the manual-operations tax" is the front-end PayFast self-purchase flow. That argues for a thin first release — migration + entitlement + PayFast + auth + Afrikaans gate — and deferring most P1. Instead P1 pulls in ratings/reviews, directory, notifications, full challenges UX, library, training, InkPols, sponsors, org pages. Bundling all of that into "launch" against solo resourcing and no date means the realistic outcome is a perpetually-near-launch build where the high-value payment fix is held hostage to sponsor-CPT scheduling logic. A skeptical stakeholder should refuse to greenlight "P0+P1" as one committed scope.
**What would fix it:** define a genuine first release around the §1 wedge (migration + payment automation + Afrikaans + read), demote most P1 to a sequenced second release, and either set a date or set a scope cap — having neither is how solo brownfield rebuilds die.

### H-4. The friendship→follow conversion contradicts itself between PRD body, journey, and migration step. (UJ-6; §3 volg; §10 "Must survive" + MR-8; Addendum E)
UJ-6 (the migration-day journey) says Johan's "old **friendships** now appear as **one-way volg** relationships." The glossary (§3) defines `volg` as "follow (one-way / asymmetric)." But MR-8 and Addendum E specify each friendship converts into **two** mutual follow records (A→B *and* B→A). Two reciprocal follows is not "one-way" — it's a faithful reproduction of symmetric friendship using two directed edges. So the *data* migration (MR-8: preserve the mutual relationship) contradicts the *narrative* (UJ-6: relationships become one-way) and the conceptual framing (§3: follow replaces friendship with asymmetry).
**Why this is high:** this is a real, undecided product question hiding inside a "resolved" migration step. Does INK want to *preserve* old mutual connections (MR-8, two edges) or *reframe* them as one-way (UJ-6, §1's "asymmetric replaces friendship")? These produce different follower/following counts on day one, different following-feeds (FR-39), and different member perceptions. The PRD asserts both. An engineer implementing MR-8 will produce a result that contradicts the journey the same PRD says it "realizes."
**What would fix it:** pick one. If mutual preservation (MR-8) is correct, fix UJ-6 and the §1/§3 "one-way replaces friendship" framing to say "existing mutual friendships are preserved as reciprocal follows; *new* relationships are one-way." If reframing is correct, fix MR-8.

### H-5. Several "testable consequences" are not testable as written; "done" is undefined for key gates. (FR-1, FR-4, NFR-6, FR-18, §16 "verified by unit test")
The PRD markets many consequences as "(testable)" but several aren't operational:
- **FR-1 / NFR-1**: "no English string appears" — against what dictionary? Afrikaans and English share many tokens (e.g., "kan," proper nouns, "PDF," "URL," member-supplied content). An automated leak scan needs a defined allowlist/heuristic; none is specified, so "zero English" has no deterministic pass/fail.
- **FR-4 / NFR-6**: "easily configurable by staff… no code change," "Site Editor stability" (NFR-6), "low, sustainable editorial effort" (§2.1) — "easily," "stability," "low," "sustainable" are unmeasurable. A stakeholder cannot dispute a claim of "easy," and an engineer cannot test it.
- **FR-18**: "plain-text + basic-formatting editor (not a full rich-text editor)" — "basic" is undefined (bold/italic? lists? links? line breaks for poetry?). For a *poetry* platform where line breaks and stanzas are load-bearing (FR-25), the editor's exact capability is a real spec gap, not a detail.
- **FR-13 "Verified by unit test"** asserts a *no-path-exists* property ("no code path where changing subscription state mutates tier"). A unit test can prove specific paths don't mutate; it cannot prove the *absence* of all such paths. The guardrail is good; the verification claim overstates what a test can guarantee.
**Why this is high:** these are the requirements an engineer builds against and a reviewer signs off against. Where "done" is an adjective ("easy," "basic," "stable"), sign-off is subjective and disputes surface at acceptance, not design.
**What would fix it:** convert adjectives to thresholds or concrete capability lists (editor: enumerate allowed marks incl. poetry line/stanza handling; "configurable": name the exact admin actions; leak scan: define the detection method and allowlist).

## MEDIUM

### M-1. POPIA, retention, and public-profile defaults are deferred — but P0/P1 features that depend on them ship at launch. (§8; §11; OQ-3; FR-1, FR-42, FR-40)
POPIA posture (lawful basis, deletion behaviour, default profile visibility) is deferred to a "founder + legal" session (OQ-3), explicitly a "pre-launch gate." Yet FR-1 (registration — the consent-capture point) is **P0**, and FR-42 (public reader ratings/reviews) and FR-40 (public profiles) are **P1 / at-launch**. You cannot build the registration consent flow (FR-1, P0) correctly before the lawful-basis decision exists, and you cannot ship public reviews/profiles before the public-visibility-and-opt-out decision exists. The PRD treats POPIA as a content gate (insert values later) when it's actually a *design* gate (it changes data model and flows).
**Why medium not critical:** it's flagged honestly as a deferral with named owners, and it's a known pre-launch blocker — but the PRD understates that it blocks *building* P0/P1, not just *launching*. A skeptical reviewer should require the OQ-3 session *before* FR-1/FR-40/FR-42 are built, not before go-live.

### M-2. The User Journeys — used to justify FRs via "Realizes UJ-n" — are admittedly assumed and unvalidated. (§2.3 preamble; OQ-11; §17)
The §2.3 preamble states the journeys are "[ASSUMPTION] on specifics… please correct any beat that misreads how a real INK session goes," and OQ-11 defers validation against real sessions to the founder. Yet FRs throughout cite "Realizes UJ-n" as traceability justification (e.g., FR-2, FR-5, FR-19, FR-26, FR-38). The traceability chain therefore rests on unvalidated fiction. If UJ-1's "no reader/writer choice at signup" beat or UJ-2's "buy R60 then immediately write" flow misreads real behaviour, the FRs they anchor inherit the error.
**Why medium:** the FRs are mostly defensible on their own merits and the assumption is labelled — but presenting "Realizes UJ-n" as validation while the UJs are unconfirmed overstates the confidence of the requirements baseline.

### M-3. "100% data preservation as a launch blocker" is an unrealistic absolute that will force a bad call on cutover day. (§15 SM-1)
SM-1: "100% of members, active intekenings, bydraes, and media survive… 100% of moved URLs return a 301," and "[ASSUMPTION: 100% is the bar; any loss is a launch blocker]." On a "several thousand post," years-old, plugin-tangled WordPress site, *some* legacy data is near-certainly corrupt, orphaned, or already broken pre-migration (dead media references, posts by deleted users — MR-4 already anticipates "writers with no WP account"). A literal 100%-or-block bar means either (a) cutover is blocked indefinitely by pre-existing legacy rot the rebuild didn't cause, or (b) the bar gets quietly waived under launch pressure, which destroys its credibility as a gate.
**Why medium:** the *intent* (preservation over convenience) is right and correctly prioritized — but an unqualified 100% absolute with no tolerance for pre-existing legacy damage is a gate that will be violated in practice. Define "100% of *valid* records; pre-existing broken data enumerated and accepted in a reconciliation log."

### M-4. PayFast is "already installed yet unused," which is asserted as low-risk but is the project's central new integration. (§1 "why now"; FR-5; OQ-9)
§1 leans on "PayFast is already installed yet unused — the missing piece is a front-end self-purchase flow." "Installed" is not "working." A gateway that has never processed a live transaction has never proven its IPN/ITN callback handling, its return-URL membership-activation wiring (FR-5: "on successful PayFast return, the membership activates automatically"), or its behaviour on the new host. FR-5's automatic activation depends on PayFast's asynchronous payment notification reaching `ink-core`/Woo correctly — a notoriously fiddly integration (callbacks, signatures, sandbox-vs-live URL config) that the PRD treats as a known quantity because the plugin is "installed."
**Why medium:** correctly scoped to sandbox-only testing (FR-5, NFR-9) and it's a vetted plugin — but "the single change that lifts the manual-operations tax" (§1) is also the single most integration-fragile new path, and the PRD's "already installed" framing understates that. The §1 thesis rests on the least-proven seam.

### M-5. "No auto-renew at launch" collides with the whole automation premise and the subscription model. (FR-4; §14.2; §1)
The entire "why now" is *automation* — replacing manual EFT. But FR-4 specifies "No auto-renew at launch," and §14.2 admits auto-renew is "emotionally load-bearing for reducing churn." So at launch every member must *manually* re-purchase at expiry (FR-8 renew flow). That means the automated system still requires a manual member action every term, and expiries (the revenue-critical event) depend on members remembering to come back and pay — exactly the kind of leak manual EFT had. The PRD automates *activation* but not *continuity*, while selling itself on automation.
**Why medium:** honestly deferred with a reason (PayFast recurring support unverified, OQ-9) — but the PRD doesn't acknowledge that launching without auto-renew partially undercuts the §1 automation thesis and likely depresses any retention/conversion metric (SM-5). At minimum it needs expiry-reminder notifications (FR-44 mentions follow/challenge alerts but not "your intekening expires soon") to not bleed members.

### M-6. The Afrikaans-leak scan is named as a CI/cron gate but its mechanism makes it unreliable for the hardest cases. (NFR-1, NFR-8; Addendum D)
The scan is "crawl key front-end pages + `wp i18n` untranslated counts." `wp i18n` counts untranslated *gettext* strings — it cannot see (a) plugin JS strings in `.json` (Addendum D's explicit leak vector), (b) plugin-*composed* sentences assembled at runtime, (c) REST/AJAX/feed output, or (d) transactional email bodies, all of which Addendum D lists as in-scope leak vectors. A crawler of "key pages" also won't reach logged-in-only states, error/validation paths, or email. So the named tooling does not cover the leak vectors the same documents say matter most.
**Why medium:** the *ambition* is right and rare — but the gate's coverage is narrower than its claim (SM-2's "zero everywhere, standing"). The hardest leaks (JS, composed sentences, emails) are precisely what the named method misses, making "zero leakage" partly unverifiable by the stated mechanism.

## LOW

### L-1. Addendum E-test contradicts the PRD's own headline registration decision. (Addendum G; FR-2; OQ-15)
The Addendum's E2E test flow (G) reads "register → **choose intent** → buy via PayFast sandbox," and Addendum B/§ list "intent" as a modelled `enum`. But FR-2 (P0) and OQ-15 explicitly *removed* the signup intent gate ("no reader/writer choice at signup," "no `ink_writer_intent` stored"). The Addendum carries a stale "choose intent" beat and a stale "intent" enum. Harmless to the build if caught, but it means the test spec and data-model enum list encode a decision the PRD reversed — an agent reading the Addendum could re-introduce the removed intent field.
**Fix:** strike "choose intent" from Addendum G and remove "intent" from the enum list in Addendum A/B.

### L-2. FR-50 priority notation is ambiguous. (FR-50; §14.1)
FR-50 is tagged "(P0 for admin, FL 11.6)" — a hybrid priority found nowhere in the P0/P1/P2 scheme defined in §4 preamble. §14.1 then lists "queryable winner records (FR-50)" flatly under P0. "P0 for admin" implies a partial-P0 (admin can record, public surfacing is later?) that the scope section flattens to full P0. Minor, but an agent sequencing epics needs to know whether the public winner display is launch-critical or not.
**Fix:** resolve to a single priority or split FR-50 into the P0 admin-record part and a later public-surface part.

### L-3. Real3D Flipbook PDF dependency for InkPols has performance/accessibility tension unaddressed. (FR-57; NFR-3, NFR-5)
InkPols is PDF-in-a-flipbook (FR-57, "no per-article extraction," §13). NFR-3 says "avoid heavy front-end JS" and NFR-5 prioritizes legibility/accessibility. A JS flipbook rendering full-issue PDFs is heavy front-end JS and is typically poor for accessibility and mobile legibility — a direct tension with NFR-3/NFR-5 that the PRD lists as separate facts without acknowledging the conflict. Also, FR-57's reliance on a niche premium plugin for a core content surface is exactly the "irreducible exposure" leak/update risk (Addendum D) for a P1 feature.
**Fix:** acknowledge the flipbook ↔ performance/accessibility tradeoff and define a minimum acceptable mobile/a11y fallback (e.g., direct PDF link).

### L-4. "No discount/savings framing" (FR-4) conflicts with offering 1/6/12-month plans whose entire economic logic is a volume discount. (FR-4)
FR-4 prices R60/1mo, R300/6mo, R600/12mo and bans "Save 12%/25%" framing. But R300/6mo (R50/mo) and R600/12mo (R50/mo) *are* discounts vs R60/mo — the longer terms are cheaper per month. The product structure embeds a discount the copy is forbidden from naming. Members will do the arithmetic; forbidding the framing doesn't remove the discount, it just removes the explanation, which can read as inconsistent rather than "non-commercial."
**Why low:** it's a tone choice (§6 "nie 'n markplek nie") and defensible — but worth noting the pricing structure and the copy rule are mildly at odds, and a stakeholder may later ask why the savings aren't stated.

## Cross-cutting observation (not a single-location finding)

The PRD's greatest strength — its relentless terminological and conflation-rule discipline — is also where it spends its rigour, leaving the **launch-stakes engineering risks (migration verification, entitlement-lifecycle testing, classification quality, dependency integration) comparatively under-specified.** The document is bulletproof on *what to call things* and noticeably softer on *how we'll know the migration actually worked weeks after cutover*. For a brownfield rebuild carrying a paying membership and "thousands of contributions," that allocation is inverted from the actual risk: nobody's launch fails because they wrote "outeur" instead of "skrywer"; launches fail because a migrated subscription silently mis-expires or a third of the library lands in a holding bucket. Re-weight verification effort toward §10 and NFR-9 before greenlighting.
